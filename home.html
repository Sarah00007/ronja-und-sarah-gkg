Historie of Cocktails

Where comeas the term 'Cocktail' from? Cocktails are often assumed to be an American invention, however they were influenced by British punches, which were large bowls of liquor blended with fruit juice, spices, and other ingredients and served at punch houses in the 18th century. Cocktail was first mentioned in a British newspaper published in March of 1798.But it wasn't until 1806, when The Balance and Columbian Repository of Hudson, New York, came up with the term "a stimulating liquor made of any kind of sugar, water, and bitters, vulgarly called a bittered sling," that the term wa properly defined as .Cocktails have always been about "stimulating," but the genuine art of bartending emerged owing to one man in particular—Jerry Thomas. Jerry, alias "Professor" Thomas, a successful American bartender from Connecticut who worked all throughout America and Europe and published one of the first comprehensive recipe books, The Bartender's Guide (or How to Mix Drinks), which became a benchmark for bartenders.

Evolution.According to the Columbian Repository, cocktails had grown into their own category of drink by the early 1800s, consisting of sugar, water, and bitters. In the early twentieth century, the introduction of refrigeration and cheaply available ice transformed the scene for bartenders, who experienced a surge in demand for iced and shaken beverages. Nowadays, the social ritual that used to surround cocktails is more important than the cocktails themselves. When I offer someone a classic like a plain old fashioned and they say, "Wow, this is like something my grandfather would have prepared," I'm reminded of this. The term "prepared" is crucial. That's because our forefathers and mothers didn't consume drinks in a fancy cocktail bar but made them themselves at home.
