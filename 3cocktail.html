Himbeer Mojito

Ingrediants:

  • (for 4 portions)
  • 250g raspberries
  • 2 strands fresh mint
  • 2 limes (washed, bio)
  • 2EL cane sugar
  • 2EL Maplesirup
  • 100 ml Rum, white
  • Crushed Ice
  • 500 ml sparkling water, classic

Sugar rim:

  • 2 plates or big enough bowls
  • sugar or optional salt
    (for use with other beverages)
  • food colouring
  • raspberry sirup
  • optional eadible glitter

How to prepare it:

  1. Wash the raspberries carefully. Set aside a handful. Purée the remaining
    raspberries with a hand blender. Pass the raspberry purée through a sieve,
    discarding the seeds.
  2. Wash the mint, shake dry and pluck off four connected leaves. Pluck the remaining
    leaves individually and divide between the glasses. Wash the limes, cut them into
    quarters and place them in the glasses together with the sugar and maple syrup.
    Lightly crush everything with a pestle so that the lime juice comes out.
  3. Divide 25 ml rum between the glasses. Mix the raspberry puree with the crushed
    ice and pour over the top. Top up with mineral water.

History of the traditional Mojito:

The nameAlthough the mojito is indeed a Cuban drink, there are several theories about how it got its name. According to one theory, the name mojito comes from the West African word mojo, which refers to a cloth bag holding magical spices and artefacts; as a diminutive (belittling) form of this loanword, mojito means "small magic." There is also a spice blend with the same name, which could be the source of the name, albeit this mojo is made with bitter orange rather than lime. The word could alternatively be derived from the Spanish verb mojar (to make something wet), or it could be a contraction of the adjectives mojadito (something wet) or mojado (wet).

First CreationThe mojito is a rum drink with a lengthy history in the Caribbean. To relieve stomach pains, British privateer Francis Drake is claimed to have drank a drink made of sugar, real limes (a sour, aromatic Caribbean kind), Aguardiente de Caa (a basic sugar cane alcohol), and mint as early as the 16th century. Drake was dubbed "El Draque" (Spanish for "dragon") by the Spaniards, and this was also the name given to the drink that was eventually blended with rum as its manufacturing spread over the Caribbean. In his tale El Cólera en Habana, published in 1838, Cuban author Rámon de Palma wrote:"Every day at eleven o'clock I take a draquecito, and it does me a lot of good."

The traditional MojitaThis mixture was not called a mojito until the beginning of the 20th century: According to author Ciro Bianchi Ross and Cuban historian Miguel Bonera, the mojito first appeared in print in 1910 and was served at La Concha in Havana. At that time, iced drinks were extremely popular and ice had long been produced commercially. The mojito finally became a legend through Ernest Hemingway, who ordered it from Angel Martinez in his regular bar La Bodeguita del Medio in Havana. One of his sayings hangs above the bar there today: "My Mojito in La Bodeguita, my Daiquiri in El Floridita".
